Before You Start

Readiness checklist, environment setup, and what to gather

✅ Thomas's Readiness Checklist
Is Thomas Ready? Check each sign
Readiness Score0%
  • Can stay dry for at least 1 hour
  • Shows awareness of being wet or soiled (pulling at diaper, signs of discomfort)
  • Has some predictable schedule (urinates around consistent times)
  • Can sit on a chair or surface for 2-3 minutes
  • Can follow a simple one-step instruction with or without gestures
  • Responds to a reward or praise (some form of positive reinforcement works)
  • Bowel movements are somewhat predictable (timing can be anticipated)
  • Can pull pants up/down with physical assistance
  • Has a preferred reward / highly motivating item identified
  • Is not currently experiencing major transitions, illness, or family stress
For Level 3 ASD: You don't need all boxes checked. If Thomas has 5+ and a consistent pattern, you can start. Nonverbal children can train successfully — communication method is less important than consistency and reinforcement.
🛒 What You Need to Gather
Shopping + Setup List
  • 🪑 Potty seat reducer / insert (so he doesn't feel like he'll fall in)
  • 🦶 Footstool under toilet for stability and proper squatting posture
  • 👖 6–10 pairs of real cotton underwear (NO pull-ups during training days)
  • 🧴 Baby wipes (if Thomas prefers wet wipes, continue — transition to paper later)
  • 🎁 Premium reward stash: Thomas's absolute FAVORITE treats/toys (reserved ONLY for potty success)
  • 🖼️ Visual schedule cards for bathroom wall (print from Visual Steps tab)
  • 📱 This app on your phone or tablet for timer + tracking
  • 🎧 Child-size noise-canceling or comfort headphones (for flush anxiety)
  • 💡 Soft nightlight or lamp (dim option) for bathroom comfort
  • 🧸 Comfort object or fidget toy for him to hold while sitting
  • 💧 Extra juice, water, milk — high fluid intake boosts practice opportunities
  • 🗒️ Accident log notebook (or use Tracker tab)
🛁 Bathroom Setup
Make the Bathroom Feel SAFE
🔇
Flush Fear
Let Thomas leave before flushing. Or flush with his hand while you cover his ears. Gradual desensitization — never force exposure to the flush sound.
💡
Lighting
Harsh fluorescent lights bother many sensory-sensitive kids. Use a warm lamp or nightlight. Soft, warm-toned light = calmer bathroom experience.
🎵
Sound
Play Thomas's favorite calm music or a preferred video quietly while he sits. This gives him something to focus on and extends sit time.
🌡️
Temperature
Cold toilet seats cause sensory shock. Use a padded seat cover. Warm the bathroom in winter. Remove sensory friction wherever you can.
🎨
Personalize It
Put Thomas's favorite characters, colors, or stickers on the bathroom door and walls. Make the bathroom HIS special space, not a scary one.
🧸
Comfort Kit
Keep a small basket near the toilet: favorite fidget toy, a book, a small tablet for videos. Reserved only for potty time — makes it special.
💊 Medical Considerations
Check These With Thomas's Pediatrician
  • ⚠️Constipation is extremely common in ASD children (restricted diets, sensory eating) — address before starting or progress will stall
  • ⚠️GI issues like pain or discomfort during elimination can look like resistance — rule out medical causes first
  • ⚠️If Thomas withholds bowel movements, consult the pediatrician ASAP — do not push through
  • 💊Miralax/fiber supplements (with MD guidance) can make bowel training significantly easier
  • 🩺Mention to his BCBA if services start — they can build a formal individualized toileting plan

The 6-Week Plan

Evidence-based phased approach for Level 3 ASD • Intensive but achievable

The research says: Structured, phase-based schedules with immediate reinforcement are the most effective method for nonverbal children with ASD. Intensive training (10-15 min intervals) in early weeks dramatically accelerates the timeline versus gradual methods.
Overview
📊 Full Timeline
Weeks 1-2 · Foundation
🏗️ Build the Habit — Every 10-15 Minutes
  • Switch to real underwear on Day 1. No exceptions during waking hours.
  • Set timer for every 10-15 minutes. Escort Thomas to bathroom every single time.
  • Use a clear, consistent verbal + visual cue: show the potty picture card and say "Potty time!"
  • Target sit time: 2-3 minutes. Never force longer — keep it positive.
  • Increase fluids significantly on training days (juice, water, milk)
  • Log every void, accident, and close call. Track when accidents happen most.
  • Immediate reward for ANY sit attempt + BIGGER reward for any success
  • Flush AFTER Thomas leaves the bathroom — gradually introduce the sound later
  • Expect many accidents. That is normal and expected at this stage.
Weeks 3-4 · Pattern Recognition
🔍 Use Data to Schedule Smarter
  • Review your accident log. Look for patterns — when does Thomas usually go?
  • Extend timer intervals to 20-30 minutes based on what data shows
  • Continue rewarding every success immediately and consistently
  • If Thomas has a "preferred void time," prioritize that window each day
  • Begin teaching hand-wash routine as part of the chain
  • Success rate should be climbing. Even 30-40% success is great progress.
  • Coordinate with Head Start — consistency across settings speeds mastery
  • Celebrate loudly and enthusiastically — victory dances, stickers, preferred treats
Weeks 5-6 · Independence Building
🌱 Extending Intervals + Reducing Prompts
  • Extend intervals to 30-45 minutes if success rate is consistent
  • Begin fading physical prompt — verbal cue only, then pause to let Thomas initiate
  • If using PECS or AAC device: practice potty icon request daily
  • Watch for any physical cues — squatting, grabbing, getting still — prompt immediately
  • Shift reward to bigger milestones (token economy: 3 stars = special reward)
  • Nighttime/nap training is secondary — focus on daytime mastery first
  • If progress has stalled, consult BCBA for an individualized protocol
  • Regression is normal after illness or stress — restart with shorter intervals
Weeks 7-12 · Maintenance (if needed)
🏁 Consolidation and Generalization
  • Practice in different bathrooms — grocery stores, Grandma's, Head Start
  • Generalization is its own skill set — don't assume home training transfers automatically
  • Continue the visual schedule in ALL settings (portable picture card)
  • 60-70% daytime success without prompting = significant milestone
  • Nighttime training: limit fluids 1 hour before bed, wake for one mid-night potty trip
  • Track bowel movements separately — bowel training often takes 2-4 weeks longer than urine

Calm Corner

Reducing anxiety around the potty — for Thomas AND for you

🧠 Why Thomas May Fear the Potty
🔊
Flush Sound
The sudden loud noise of flushing is unpredictable and startling. One of the most common potty fears in ASD.
🕳️
Fear of Falling
The regular toilet seat feels huge and unstable. Children fear falling in. A seat reducer + footstool eliminates this.
🧊
Cold Surfaces
Cold toilet seat = sensory shock = fight-or-flight. Padded seat covers are a simple fix that makes a huge difference.
💡
Harsh Lighting
Bright fluorescent bathroom lights overwhelm sensory processing. Soft warm light creates a calmer environment.
🔄
Loss of Control
Going somewhere new for an uncomfortable purpose feels out of control. Routine + predictability restore the sense of safety.
🧸
Missing Activities
Fear of missing preferred activities while in the bathroom. Make a "PAUSED" sign or keep a preferred item in the bathroom.
🎯 Desensitization Steps
Gradual Exposure Protocol — Don't Rush This
Phase 1 · Days 1-3
Bathroom Visits With No Pressure
Bring Thomas to the bathroom just to hang out. Play in there. Put his favorite toys in there. No potty expectation at all. Just: bathroom = safe place.
Phase 2 · Days 4-6
Sit on Closed Toilet (Clothed)
Have Thomas sit on the toilet lid with clothes on. Big rewards for sitting. No pants-down yet. Just getting comfortable with the seat and position.
Phase 3 · Day 7+
Pants Down on Seat
Introduce the actual sitting position. Keep sessions short (30 seconds to start). Reward every attempt. Use preferred fidget toy or screen during sit.
Phase 4 · Ongoing
Flush Desensitization
Let Thomas practice flushing alone with no pee/poop involved. Let him leave first, then flush while he's outside. Gradually move to flushing together while wearing headphones. Never force.

🧠 Reinforcement Science
What the Research Says About Rewards
  • ⚡Immediacy is everything. A reward given 30 seconds later does NOT reinforce the same behavior for nonverbal ASD children. It must be within 3 seconds.
  • 🎯Find the "nuclear" motivator. Not just something he likes — something he absolutely loves. Reserve it ONLY for potty success to maintain its power.
  • 📈Increase fluids to increase opportunities. More successful voids = more rewards = faster reinforcement learning. Give extra juice on training days.
  • 🔄Reward every tinkle. Early on, each single void earns a full reward. Don't wait for a "big" success. Volume doesn't matter — behavior does.
  • 🔁Vary the reward slightly. Rotating between 2-3 preferred items prevents satiation (when the reward loses its power).
  • 📉Fade rewards gradually. Once success is consistent, shift to intermittent reinforcement and social praise. Don't remove rewards abruptly.

Tips & Science

Research-backed strategies for Level 3 nonverbal ASD

🔬 The Big Findings
👖
Underwear, Not Pull-Ups
Research strongly supports immediate switch to real underwear. Modern pull-ups are TOO good — they wick away moisture so effectively that children don't feel wet, removing the sensory signal that drives learning. (Autism Speaks ATN, 2016)
⏱️
Scheduled Sits Work Best
Starting every 10-15 minutes (with gradual fading) is the most evidence-backed approach for ASD children who don't show typical "need to go" signals. The schedule replaces what the body cue can't yet communicate. (MasterMind Behavior, 2025)
💧
Increase Fluids Strategically
On intensive training days, increase fluid intake. More voids = more chances for reinforced successes. Autism Speaks recommends tracking void times 3-5 days before starting to find natural patterns. (Autism Speaks, 2016)
🖼️
Visual Supports Over Verbal
For nonverbal children, picture cues are dramatically more effective than verbal instructions. Present the visual cue simultaneously with a brief verbal prompt. Simultaneous presentation beats sequential. (Autism Speaks, ABA research consensus)
🔗
Chaining the Behavior
ABA task analysis breaks toileting into 8-10 links. Each step is taught explicitly. Forward chaining (teach step 1 first) or backward chaining (teach the last step first for faster reward) both work. Backward chaining can accelerate progress. (ABA consensus)
🔄
Accident Protocol Matters
NEVER punish accidents. Neutral response only: "Oops, accident. Let's go potty." Then immediately walk to the toilet and have a brief sit. This connects the wet feeling to the correct action. (Regency ABA, 2026)
🏫
Consistency Across Settings
Generalization is a distinct skill set. Once Thomas succeeds at home, the skill must be explicitly taught in each new bathroom. Bring the visual cards to Head Start. Coordinate with teachers. Home success does NOT automatically transfer. (Cleveland Clinic, 2026)
💩
Bowel Training Is Separate
Most ASD children master urine training before bowel training — often by several weeks. Treat them as separate goals. Constipation (extremely common in ASD due to restricted diets) must be medically addressed or bowel training will fail. (Learning Tree ABA, 2024)
❓ Common Questions
What if Thomas has a complete meltdown about the potty? ▼
Stop the current attempt immediately — you cannot successfully train through a full meltdown. Return to the desensitization protocol in the Calm Corner. Go back to "no pressure" bathroom visits. The bathroom must feel safe before training can progress. This is not failure — it's critical information about where you need to start. Never force sitting. Never restrain. Never punish. Consult with Thomas's BCBA if one is active.
How do we handle training at Head Start? ▼
Share this plan and Thomas's visual schedule with his teachers and aides. Request that they implement the same scheduled sits during school hours. Bring a portable picture card strip for his classroom bathroom. The more consistent the approach across settings, the faster mastery comes. Ask Rochelle at NWOR to document the toileting plan as part of his IEP accommodations so it's protected.
Should we use a small potty chair or the big toilet? ▼
Start with whichever creates less fear. A small standalone potty chair is less intimidating but requires re-learning on the big toilet later. A toilet seat reducer with footstool is preferred by most ABA practitioners because it generalizes — Thomas learns on the same toilet he'll use at school and other settings. Either is fine; pick based on Thomas's comfort and then commit to it consistently.
What if we see no progress after 2 weeks? ▼
First, rule out medical issues — constipation, GI discomfort, or UTI can stall training completely. Second, review your data: Are intervals too long? Is the reward truly motivating? Is there a sensory barrier you haven't identified? Third, consider contacting Thomas's pediatrician and requesting a referral to a BCBA with toileting experience. Two weeks of intensive, consistent training with zero success (not just slow success — zero) is a signal to get professional eyes on the protocol.
How does PECS / AAC fit into potty training? ▼
If Thomas has a PECS system or AAC device, add a "toilet" or "potty" icon immediately. Practice exchanging the icon as a request during scheduled sits — even if he doesn't initiate on his own yet. Eventually, the goal is for Thomas to use the icon to REQUEST a potty trip before an accident. This self-initiation skill is the final milestone. Communication method doesn't need to be verbal — the icon exchange IS communication and it works.
Our 12 acres — is outdoor peeing a problem? ▼
Yes, this can interfere — if Thomas discovers that outdoor peeing is acceptable, it can compete with indoor toilet training. Keep a consistent rule during the training period: all pee goes in the toilet, indoors. If outdoor accidents happen, respond with the same neutral protocol and immediately guide him inside to a quick sit. Don't make outdoor space a bathroom alternative during active training.
